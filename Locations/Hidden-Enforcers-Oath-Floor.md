# Hidden Enforcers Oath Floor

## Overview

**The Oath Floor** is the Hidden Enforcers' secret initiatory headquarters beneath [Seven Directions Dojo](Seven-Directions-Dojo.md). Publicly, the lower level is a condemned basement and storm-shelter remnant. In practice, it is a warded meeting hall, oath chamber, training floor, and records vault used for private Hidden Enforcers business.

The complex is hidden below the public dojo through a locked stairwell concealed behind mat storage. It is built into older reinforced basement infrastructure, likely predating the current business, and has been renovated into a deliberately plain underground base: concrete walls, old brass fixtures, shielded lockers, utility conduits, and a central mat marked with a seven-point compass motif. The site is meant to feel less like a temple and more like a precinct basement that learned how to keep magical secrets.

## Operating role

The Oath Floor supports several overlapping functions:

- Initiatory meetings and oath instruction for the Hidden Enforcers.
- Controlled sparring, discipline training, and physical-adept mentoring.
- Confidential civic-enforcement briefings away from public police or city systems.
- Secure storage for sensitive records, trophies, evidence, and magical objects.
- Emergency fallback space for trusted members who need to disappear from public view for a few hours.

### 1. Concealed stair and old storm door

The public entrance is the locked stairwell behind Seven Directions' mat-storage wall. To ordinary dojo students, it is an old condemned-basement access point used only for building maintenance. The door has mundane locks, an alarm contact, and enough grime around it to make disinterest believable.

The stairwell descends through a narrow concrete throat to an old storm door at the bottom. This second door is the true threshold. Members treat crossing it as a reminder that they are leaving civilian cover behind and entering the order's confidence.

**Function:** Screening choke point, mundane access control, and symbolic boundary between the cover business and the hidden headquarters.

### 2. Intake antechamber

The antechamber is a low-ceilinged room with a bench, a wall clock, a battered first-aid cabinet, and three plain lockers for visitors. Guests wait here until a member escorts them deeper inside. The room is intentionally uncomfortable: no windows, no decoration beyond old safety signage, and no clear view into the rest of the complex.

A recessed desk can be unfolded from one wall for signing paper logs or taking custody of phones, weapons, credsticks, and obvious electronics before a visitor proceeds.

**Function:** Visitor holding, property control, initial questioning, and delay space if the site needs time to lock down.

### 3. The Seven-Point Hall

The Seven-Point Hall is the central training and assembly room. It is an open concrete chamber with a reinforced ceiling, removable mats, and a seven-point compass motif painted into the center of the floor. The marks divide the room into stations used for instruction, oath recitation, and controlled sparring.

During normal closed meetings, members stand by station rather than rank. During training, the mats convert the chamber into a practical fighting floor. During initiatory work, the lights are lowered and the compass points become ritual positions.

**Function:** Main meeting room, oath chamber, initiatory floor, and controlled sparring space.

### 4. Briefing alcove / watch desk

A glassless interior window overlooks the Seven-Point Hall from a shallow briefing alcove. The space holds a battered conference table, wall maps of Nashville, an old evidence board, and a locked cabinet of paper forms that look like obsolete police paperwork at first glance.

This is where trusted members quietly compare civic problems, magical disturbances, and off-book enforcement concerns before deciding whether the Hidden Enforcers should intervene. Nothing here is meant to look glamorous. It resembles the back room of an underfunded precinct.

**Function:** Mission planning, local-threat triage, and internal briefings.

### 5. Records vault

The records vault is a dry, climate-controlled archive lined with compact shelving and dull gray file cabinets. It holds paper membership ledgers, oath records, incident summaries, old case clippings, and dead-drop instructions preserved outside vulnerable public systems.

Some records are intentionally mundane: attendance sheets, dues notes, maintenance invoices, and old class rosters from Seven Directions. Others are sensitive enough that the Hidden Enforcers keep them offline by habit.

**Function:** Paper archive, membership continuity, black-file storage, and evidence preservation.

### 6. The Reliquary vault

The Reliquary is the Oath Floor's high-security artifact vault. It is designed for high-powered magical objects, especially weapon foci, confiscated talismans, dangerous trophies, and ritual tools too volatile or politically sensitive to leave in an evidence room.

The door is a heavy, inward-opening vault slab set behind a false utility panel. Inside, objects are separated into individual lockers, each with a paper tag, custody history, and a plain-language hazard note. The room is deliberately small so no one can comfortably draw, swing, or test a stored weapon focus inside it.

Typical contents might include:

- Unclaimed or confiscated weapon foci awaiting judgment by senior members.
- Seized ritual blades, badges, rings, charms, and oath-marked tools.
- Evidence from magical crimes that could compromise victims or officers if logged through normal channels.
- Legacy objects whose owners died, vanished, or broke faith with the order.

**Function:** Secure artifact storage, chain-of-custody isolation, magical hazard containment, and plot hook reservoir.

### 7. Armory and restraint cage

Separate from the Reliquary, the mundane armory holds batons, shock gloves, restraints, armor vests, old shields, training weapons, and emergency medical restraints. This room is practical rather than ceremonial; it exists so members can equip for ugly civic work without exposing the Reliquary or pulling gear through the public dojo.

A locked inner cage contains restricted tools that require two members to sign out together.

**Function:** Mundane equipment storage, response gear, and controlled issue of restraint tools.

### 8. Quiet room / cleansing room

The quiet room is a small chamber with a drain in the floor, washable walls, a sink, cheap towels, and two chairs facing away from each other. Members use it after violent encounters, magical contamination scares, or oath discipline sessions. It can also serve as a private place to calm down a recruit before or after a difficult initiation step.

The room is not comfortable, but it is mercifully plain. The Hidden Enforcers built it to prevent shaken members from carrying blood, panic, or astral filth directly back into the public dojo above.

**Function:** Decompression, cleanup, post-incident grounding, and private correction.

### 9. Infirmary nook

The infirmary nook sits beside the quiet room and looks like a stripped-down athletic treatment bay: cot, folding screen, old diagnostic monitor, trauma kit, splints, clean water, and locked pharmaceuticals. Maribel Soto's public physical-therapy cover gives the site a plausible reason to possess legitimate rehab and first-aid supplies upstairs, while the lower nook handles injuries that cannot be explained cleanly.

**Function:** Emergency care, post-sparring treatment, and stabilization before moving someone elsewhere.

### 10. Utility spine and emergency crawl

A narrow utility spine runs behind the main rooms, carrying old conduit, sump equipment, and backup power. It is loud, cramped, and unpleasant by design. A concealed crawl route leads toward an exterior service hatch several properties away, but it is not a comfortable escape path and may be partly flooded after heavy rain.

**Function:** Maintenance access, backup systems, and last-ditch emergency egress.

## Security / access

- Access requires sponsorship or explicit invitation from a trusted Hidden Enforcers member.
- Mundane concealment is layered with social screening through the dojo cover business.
- The public basement explanation is plausible enough for inspectors and neighbors, but the lower access is not casual; guests must be brought down by someone trusted.
- The intake antechamber and storm door create a deliberate delay zone before anyone reaches the main hall.
- The records vault, Reliquary vault, and armory are separately locked so a compromise of the meeting floor does not expose everything valuable.
- Magical and Matrix security remain provisional until the site appears in play, but the current concept assumes at least basic warding, low wireless profile, and member-only custody procedures.
