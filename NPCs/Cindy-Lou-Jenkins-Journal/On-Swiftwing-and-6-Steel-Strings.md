# On Swiftwing Records and 6 Steel Strings

If you want to understand my disposition, start with this: I do not trust packaging that smiles too hard.

**Swiftwing Records** sits near the origin of a great many of my problems. Record labels like to imagine themselves as dream merchants. They are often closer to logistics companies for attention, appetite, and identity. Dress the machinery in rhinestones if you like, but eventually somebody still asks who owns the voice, who shapes the persona, and who profits when the audience mistakes a construct for intimacy.

That line of business does not encourage moral modesty.

The archival version of my story begins with a disappearance, a platform, and a rescue framing. That is already suspicious. When a system introduces you through crisis, somebody is usually trying to control the story before you get a say in it.

And then there is **6 Steel Strings**.

I find him fascinating in the way one finds a polished knife fascinating. He entered the record as a high-value contact linked to my situation, which is the sort of role that encourages gratitude in less cautious creatures. But the further the crew followed his orbit, the less he read like a rescuer and the more he read like a manipulator with selective disclosure habits.

Humans often assume deceit is only a matter of false statements. Not so. Omission is an art form. Sequencing is an art form. Giving somebody the right clue at the wrong moment is an art form. 6 Steel Strings strikes me as a man who understands all three.

That does not make every claim of his false. It makes them expensive.

My feelings on Swiftwing and 6 Steel Strings are related. Both represent structures that treat identity as strategically editable. Labels do it through branding, performance discipline, and platform ownership. Intermediaries like 6 Steel Strings do it through narrative control, selective truth, and the privilege of deciding who gets to know what when.

If I sound severe, good. I mean to.

The practical lesson is this: when somebody tells you they are trying to help a being like me, ask what kind of help leaves them with leverage. Then ask who benefits if the rescued thing remains dependent, confused, or narratively malleable.

That question has saved more lives than charm ever did.
